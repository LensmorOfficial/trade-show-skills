# End-to-End Event Lifecycle

How the trade-show-skills repo fits together across a full event cycle.

> **Note**: The company and contacts in this example are fictional. Any resemblance to real organizations is coincidental.

This guide follows:
- **6 core lifecycle skills** that carry a show from selection to follow-up
- **4 supporting skills** that plug into planning and on-site execution

---

## Scenario

**Company**: Vantara Systems — a B2B SaaS company selling production scheduling software to food and beverage manufacturers. Mid-market focus, average deal size EUR80K-EUR200K, 6-9 month sales cycle.

**Team**: 3 people attending: 1 sales lead, 1 solutions engineer, 1 marketing manager.

**Goal**: Generate 8-12 qualified leads, sharpen differentiation against better-funded competitors, and validate demand for a new line-balancing module currently in beta.

**Target show**: Anuga FoodTec (Cologne).

---

## Phase 1 — Pre-Show

### 1. Choose the right show with `trade-show-finder`

The team starts by comparing Anuga FoodTec and ProPak Europe for a DACH / Benelux expansion plan.

**Output**: A show-selection memo with fit score, exhibit/attend decision, tradeoffs, and handoff into budget planning.

**What carries forward**: Selected show, buyer profile, and why this event is worth doing.

### 2. Map the threat landscape with `pre-show-competitor-analysis`

Once Anuga FoodTec is selected, the team asks which direct and adjacent competitors will likely matter at the show.

**Output**: Primary threats, white-space opportunities, and messaging angles to prepare before launch.

**What carries forward**: Competitive briefing points for messaging, script prep, and on-site observation priorities.

### 3. Validate the economics with `trade-show-budget-planner`

The marketing lead builds the ROI case for a 20sqm booth and 3-person team.

**Output**: Itemized budget, break-even logic, and lead target assumptions.

**What carries forward**: Approval memo, budget guardrails, and success metrics for the trip.

### 4. Turn strategy into execution with `exhibitor-checklist-generator`

After budget approval, the team generates a phased checklist with deadlines and owners.

**Output**: Action list for graphics, travel, lead capture, staff prep, shipping, and show-week operations.

**What carries forward**: Clear ownership and timeline, so execution does not depend on memory.

### 5. Drive pre-booked meetings with `booth-invitation-writer`

The sales lead drafts invites for customers, warm prospects, and cold accounts.

**Output**: Segmented invite sequences and reminder copy.

**What carries forward**: Confirmed booth meetings and message consistency for the on-site team.

### 6. Finalize swag with `booth-giveaway-planner`

The team decides what to give broadly, what to reserve for qualified leads, and how the giveaway should reinforce the product story.

**Output**: Giveaway mix, budget split, and distribution plan.

**What carries forward**: On-site visitor experience and lead qualification incentives.

---

## Phase 2 — On-Site

### 7. Prep staff with `booth-script-generator`

Before doors open, the team generates booth conversation scripts for cold walk-ups, warm leads, competitor customers, and current customers.

**Output**: Opening lines, 30-second pitch, qualification questions, and CTA guidance.

**What carries forward**: More consistent booth conversations and cleaner qualification downstream.

### 8. Qualify leads with `badge-qualifier`

After strong conversations, and again at the end of the day, the team classifies leads into Hot / Warm / Cold with explicit unknowns preserved.

**Output**: Structured lead cards with next-step recommendations.

**What carries forward**: A tiered follow-up queue that is usable the minute the team lands.

### 9. Capture competitor intel with `competitor-radar`

The solutions engineer logs competitor booth visits, brochure claims, and overheard pricing notes with source tagging.

**Output**: Trusted field-intel notes for product, sales, and leadership.

**What carries forward**: Verified observations, open questions, and counter-messaging follow-ups.

---

## Phase 3 — Post-Show

### 10. Convert conversations with `post-show-followup`

Within 24-48 hours of leaving the show, the sales lead turns the tiered lead list into differentiated follow-up sequences.

**Output**: Hot / Warm / Cold sequences matched to actual signal strength.

**What carries forward**: Faster pipeline conversion and less post-show memory loss.

---

## Core Workflow Summary

```text
trade-show-finder
  -> pre-show-competitor-analysis
  -> trade-show-budget-planner
  -> exhibitor-checklist-generator
  -> booth-invitation-writer
  -> booth-giveaway-planner
  -> booth-script-generator
  -> badge-qualifier
  -> competitor-radar
  -> post-show-followup
```

## Why This System Works

- `trade-show-finder` decides whether the show is worth doing at all
- `pre-show-competitor-analysis` sharpens positioning before the team is already on the floor
- planning skills translate strategy into budget, checklist, outreach, and giveaway execution
- on-site skills preserve context while the signal is still fresh
- `post-show-followup` converts that preserved context into timely follow-up

If any one of these handoffs breaks, the whole event program gets noisier:
- wrong show selected
- weak positioning on the floor
- untrained staff
- badge scans with no usable context
- follow-up too late or too generic
